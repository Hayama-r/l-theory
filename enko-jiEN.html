DESCRIPTION

Enko-ji Temple was originally established as a school in 1601 by order of Tokugawa Ieyasu, one of Japan’s three “Great Unifiers”, in the Fushimi area of present-day southern Kyoto. Unusual for the time, admission was open to both monks and laymen. The school also published several books, and 50,000 of Japan's oldest wooden typefaces used at the school are still preserved today. There is a small exhibition including them, which anyone interested in the history of printing and publishing in Japan should see. The temple itself was once moved to Shokokuji Temple before moving to its current location in 1667. In addition to the above-mentioned typefaces, the treasures of Enko-ji include a seated statue of the thousand-armed Kanon Bodhisattva said to be the work of famous sculptor Unkei, a statue of Zen Master Genkitsu, who founded Enko-ji and was also the school’s first headmaster, and a pair of screen paintings depicting bamboo forests. All but the seated statue of the Kanon Bodhisattva are designated as important national cultural properties; the screens can also be seen in the exhibition. A Bodhisattva is a person who is on their way to becoming a Buddha, but has not attained full enlightenment yet.

After entering the temple grounds, follow this path to the Honryu-tei garden.

The white stones in the Honryu-tei garden are arranged in a variety of patterns, symbolising a dragon’s movements while flying through the skies. The exhibition is in the building in the background.

Following the path through the inner gate you will find yourself in the Jugyu Garden, covered in moss and rich in Japanese maple trees. This gives the garden a fresh and lush green during Spring and Summer and makes it a deep red during Autumn. Furthermore, the pond in the garden is the oldest in the northern parts of Kyoto city.

Upon entering the Jugyu Garden, there is a large bowl with water and some bamboo poles, which is called “Suikinkutsu”. If you hold your ear against the opening of one of the vertical poles, you can hear flowing water.

Venturing further ahead from the Jugyu Garden, you will find yourself at the foot of a mountain. Do not turn around now, because you will be rewarded with a view over the northern parts of the city all the way to the western mountains if you make the effort and climb the stairs for just a bit. After visiting this temple, also consider going to Shisen-do, which is close by.
